Cyber
Threats

Pharming, phishing and social engineering — understand how attacks work and how to stay protected.

Pharming Phishing Social Engineering

Pharming

Malware that silently redirects genuine URLs to fake websites.

What is Pharming?

Pharming is a cyber attack where malicious software secretly installed on a victim's device redirects them to a fake website — even when they type the correct URL.

The name combines phishing + farming. Perpetrators "harvest" data from many victims simultaneously.

—

Key danger: Unlike phishing, no suspicious link needs to be clicked. Visiting certain pages or opening a rogue email attachment can silently install the redirect malware.

How a Pharming Attack Works

1

Perpetrator tricks victim via email attachment, pop-up or link.

2

Malware secretly downloaded and installed.

3

Malware alters how URLs are resolved — genuine URLs redirect silently to fake sites.

4

Victim types correct URL (e.g. their bank) but lands on a fake look-alike.

5

Victim enters credentials — immediately captured by perpetrator.

Protection

  • Only download software from trusted sources
  • Verify the URL in the address bar is exactly correct before entering any details
  • Check for HTTPS and the padlock icon
  • Run anti-malware software regularly
  • If something feels wrong, close the site immediately
—

If you entered details on a fake site, change your passwords immediately on the real site and any other accounts using the same password.

Phishing

Fake emails designed to steal personal data.

What is Phishing?

Phishing uses fake emails designed to look like they come from trusted organisations — banks, social media platforms, government agencies. The goal is to trick victims into providing personal data.

Named after "fishing" — cast many fake emails as bait, wait for victims to bite.

—

Goal: steal usernames, passwords, credit card numbers — used for fraud and identity theft.

Common Tactics

!

"Unusual account activity detected" — log in immediately to verify.

!

"You've won a prize" — click here to claim and enter your details.

!

"Confirm your recent purchase" — click to dispute this transaction.

!

"Tax refund available" — enter bank details to receive payment.

How to Spot a Phishing Email

Check the email

  • Spelling mistakes and grammar errors
  • Generic greeting: "Dear Customer" rather than your name
  • Hover over links (don't click) — check the real destination URL
  • Sender email domain doesn't match the real company

Check the website (if you clicked)

  • URL in address bar exactly correct?
  • HTTPS and padlock present?
  • Site looks identical to the real one?
—

Legitimate organisations never ask for passwords or credit card details via email links. When in doubt — navigate to the real site by typing the URL yourself.

Social Engineering

Exploiting human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering manipulates and deceives people rather than hacking systems. Perpetrators exploit human psychology — trust, fear, helpfulness — to obtain confidential information.

Phishing is one form. But social engineering also happens in person and by phone.

—

The vulnerability being exploited is human behaviour, not a software bug. Technology alone cannot fully defend against it.

Real-World Examples

Example 1 — Fake IT Technician

A perpetrator calls an employee claiming to be from IT, says there is a problem on their computer, and asks for login details to fix it remotely. An employee with a genuine IT issue that day may believe the call and hand over credentials.

—

Legitimate IT staff never ask for your password. Hang up and call your IT department on a known number.

Example 2 — The Friendly Stranger

Over several meetings at a café, a perpetrator builds rapport and casually asks about company systems, data storage and security. The employee thinks it is friendly conversation. The perpetrator is building a profile to attack the network.

Example 3 — Social Media Quizzes

Fun quizzes asking for your first pet's name, first car, favourite teacher etc. — many people use exactly this as password components. Completing these gives perpetrators a list for brute-force attacks.

Protection

  • Use access levels — employees see only the data they need
  • Train employees to recognise manipulation tactics
  • Never share passwords — even with people claiming to be IT staff
  • Set social media privacy settings to maximum
  • Do not fill in social media quizzes asking personal questions
  • Verify identity independently before providing any information

Key differences and shared characteristics.

Common exam mistake: confusing pharming and phishing. Pharming = malware redirects your device. Phishing = fake email. Social engineering = broader category including phishing and others.

Chapter 5 — Summary

All three threats at a glance.

Pharming — what

Malware redirects genuine URL to fake site silently.

Pharming — aim

Steal passwords and payment details for fraud or identity theft.

Pharming — protection

Anti-malware, check HTTPS/URL carefully.

Phishing — what

Fake email pretending to be a trusted organisation; link to fake site.

Phishing — aim

Steal login credentials and financial details.

Phishing — protection

Check spelling, personalisation, hover links, verify HTTPS.

Social Eng. — what

Manipulating people rather than systems to reveal confidential info.

Social Eng. — aim

Data for fraud, identity theft or corporate espionage.

Social Eng. — protection

Access levels, employee training, privacy settings, never share passwords.

Key distinction

Pharming = malware on device. Phishing = fake email. Social engineering = exploits humans directly.

Glossary

Key terms from Chapter 5.

PharmingA cyber attack where malware on a user's device redirects a genuine URL to a fake website.
PhishingA cyber attack using fake emails designed to look genuine, tricking users into providing personal data.
Social engineeringManipulating people — rather than systems — into revealing confidential information.
MalwareMalicious software designed to damage, disrupt or gain unauthorised access to a system.
Anti-malwareSoftware that scans for malicious files, quarantines and removes them.
HTTPSHypertext Transfer Protocol Secure — encrypted web communication, shown as a padlock in the browser.
Access levelThe data and resources a user is permitted to access, linked to their role in an organisation.
Identity theftUsing stolen personal data to impersonate the victim, typically for financial gain.
Brute-force attackTrying many password combinations until the correct one is found.
